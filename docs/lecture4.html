CT Fourier Analysis

Recall that periodic CT signals with period TT that meet the Direchlet conditions

  • The signal has a finite number of discontinuities per period.

  • The signal has a finite number of maxima and minima per period.

  • The signal is bounded, i.e. ∫T|x(t)|dt<∞\int_{T} |x(t)| \;dt < \infty

can be decomposed using the CT Fourier series

x(t)≈∑k=−∞∞akejkω0tx(t) \approx \sum\limits_{k = -\infty}^{\infty} a_k \, e^{j k\omega_0 t}

where ak∈ℂa_k \in\mathbb{C} and ω0=2πT\omega_0 = \frac{2\pi}{T}. The Fourier series coefficients are given by

ak=1T0∫0T0x(t)e−jkω0ta_k = \frac{1}{T_0} \int\limits_{0}^{T_0} x(t)e^{-jk\omega_0 t}

and measure the similarity of x(t)x(t) to the signal e−jkω0te^{-jk\omega_0 t}.

A plot of |ak||a_k| and ∠ak\angle a_k as a function of kk is called magnitude and phase spectrum.

Example. Recall that sampling of a CT signal can be thought of as multiplication by the impulse train

x(t)=∑m=−∞∞δ(t−mT)x(t) = \sum\limits_{m = -\infty}^{\infty} \delta(t-mT)

The Forier series coefficients are

ak=1T∫Tx(t)e−jkω0tdt=1T∫−T2T2δ(t)e−jkω0tdt=1Ta_k = \frac{1}{T} \int\limits_{T} x(t)e^{-jk\omega_0 t} \; dt = \frac{1}{T} \int\limits_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}} \delta(t)e^{-jk\omega_0 t} \; dt = \frac{1}{T}

For non-periodic CT signals the decomposition is over an uncountably infinite set of frequencies

x(t)=ℱ−1{X(ω)}=12π∫−∞∞X(ω)ejωtdωx(t) = \mathcal{F}^{-1}\{X(\omega)\} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int\limits_{-\infty}^{\infty} X(\omega)e^{j\omega t}\; d\omega where the Fourier transform is

X(ω)=ℱ{x(t)}=∫−∞∞x(t)e−jωtdtX(\omega) = \mathcal{F}\{x(t)\} = \int\limits_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t) e^{-j\omega t}\; dt

A plot of |X(jω)||X(j\omega)| and ∠X(jω)\angle X(j\omega) are called the magnitude and phase spectra respectively.

The most usefull property of the CTFT is the convolution property. Let

ℱ{h(t)}=H(jω)\mathcal{F}\left\{ h(t) \right\} = H(j\omega) be the Fourier transform of the impulse response of a stable system, and ℱ{x(t)}=X(jω)\mathcal{F}\left\{ x(t) \right\} = X(j\omega) be the Fourier transform of the input signal. Then the output in the Fourier domain Y(jω)Y(j\omega) is

Y(jω)=H(jω)X(jω)Y(j\omega) = H(j\omega)X(j\omega)

The output in the time domain is then the inverse Fourier transform of YY

y(t)=ℱ−1{Y(jω)}y(t) = \mathcal{F}^{-1}\left\{ Y(j\omega) \right\}

Note for this to work the Fourier transforms of h(t)h(t) and x(t)x(t) must exist.

H)jω)H)j\omega) is called the Frequency response of the system and exists if the system is BIBO stable.

This gives us an alternate route to analysis of stable systems.

DT Fourier Analysis

DT periodic functions can be decomposed as

x[n]=∑k=N0N0+N−1akejkω0nx[n] = \sum\limits_{k = N_0}^{N_0 + N-1} a_k e^{jk\omega_0n} where NN is the period, ω0=2πN\omega_0 = \frac{2\pi}{N}, and N0N_0 is any starting index for the sum.

The FT Fourier series coefficients aka_k are given by

ak=1N∑n=N0N0+N−1x[n]e−j2πNkna_k = \frac{1}{N} \sum\limits_{n = N_0}^{N_0+N-1} x[n]e^{-j\frac{2\pi}{N} k n }

As before the plot of |ak||a_k| and ∠ak\angle a_k is called the magnitude and phase spectrum.

Recall that the DTFS is proportional to the DFT/FFT X[k]X[k] of a finite-length sequence equivalent to the values of the periodic signall over just one period.

X[k]=∑n=0N−1x[n]e−j2πNknX[k] = \sum\limits_{n = 0}^{N-1} x[n]e^{-j\frac{2\pi}{N} k n }

  2. Determine the Fourier series coefficients. Let N0=0N_0= 0, then from the definition ak=1N∑n=N0N0+N−1x[n]e−j2πNkn=13∑n=02x[n]e−j2π3kn=13[x[0]e−j2π3k(0)+x[1]e−j2π3k(1)+x[2]e−j2π3k(2)]=13[1+2e−j2π3k+e−j4π3k]\begin{aligned} a_k &= \frac{1}{N} \sum\limits_{n = N_0}^{N_0+N-1} x[n]e^{-j\frac{2\pi}{N} k n }\\ &= \frac{1}{3} \sum\limits_{n = 0}^{2} x[n]e^{-j\frac{2\pi}{3} k n }\\ &= \frac{1}{3}\left[ x[0]e^{-j\frac{2\pi}{3} k (0)} + x[1]e^{-j\frac{2\pi}{3} k (1)} + x[2]e^{-j\frac{2\pi}{3} k (2)}\right]\\ &= \frac{1}{3}\left[ 1 + 2e^{-j\frac{2\pi}{3} k} + e^{-j\frac{4\pi}{3} k}\right] \end{aligned}

For non-periodic signals the decompostion is

x[n]=12π∫2πX(ejω)ejωndωx[n] = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{2\pi} X\left(e^{j\omega}\right) e^{j\omega n} \; d\omega where the integral is over any 2π2\pi period of XX. The DT Fourier transform is given by

X(ejω)=∑n=−∞∞x[n]e−jωnX\left(e^{j\omega}\right) = \sum\limits_{n = -\infty}^{\infty} x[n] e^{-j\omega n} Note X(ejω)X\left(e^{j\omega}\right) must be a periodic function with period 2π2\pi.

Again a plot of |X(ejω)||X\left(e^{j\omega}\right)| and ∠X(ejω)\angle X\left(e^{j\omega}\right) is the spectrum of the signal. It is often only plotted from [−π,π][-\pi, \pi] or [0,2π][0, 2\pi] since it is periodic outside that range and thus redundant information.

As in CT the most usefull property is related to convolution. Let ℱ{h[n]}=H(ejω)\mathcal{F}\left\{ h[n]\right\} = H\left(e^{j\omega}\right) be the DT Fourier transform of the system impulse response. Let

ℱ{x[n]}=X(ejω)\mathcal{F}\left\{ x[n]\right\} = X\left(e^{j\omega}\right) be the DT Fourier transform of the input. Then the output in the Fourier domain is given by

Y(ejω)=H(ejω)X(ejω)Y\left(e^{j\omega}\right) = H\left(e^{j\omega}\right) X\left(e^{j\omega}\right)

and in the time domain

y[n]=ℱ−1{Y(ejω)}y[n] = \mathcal{F}^{-1}\left\{ Y\left(e^{j\omega}\right) \right\}

as long as all the transforms exist.

The function H(ejω)H\left(e^{j\omega}\right) is called the frequency response of the DT system and exists if the system is BIBO stable.

This gives an alternate analysis route for DT systems, parallel to the CT case
