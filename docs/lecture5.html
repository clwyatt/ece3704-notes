Today’s lecture is a review of material from ECE 2714. We will review sampling theory, the Nyquist rate, practical sampling, and reconstruction techniques.

Recall that hybrid systems are ones with both CT and DT signals. Such systems require the ability to:

  • convert signals from CT to DT: sampling or analog-to-digital conversion (ADC)

  • convert signals from DT to CT: reconstruction or digital-to-analog conversion (DAC)

Ideal Sampling Theory

The impulse train

xp(t)=∑n=−∞∞δ(t−nT)x_p(t) = \sum\limits_{n = -\infty}^{\infty} \delta(t-nT)

is a periodic function with period TT. It has a CTFS representation

xp(t)=∑k=−∞∞akej2πTktx_p(t) = \sum\limits_{k = -\infty}^{\infty} a_k e^{j\frac{2\pi}{T} k t}

where ak=1Ta_k = \frac{1}{T} are the coefficients.

Its Fourier Transform is then

Xp(jω)=∑k=−∞∞δ(ω−2πTk)X_p(j\omega) = \sum\limits_{k = -\infty}^{\infty} \delta\left(\omega-\frac{2\pi}{T} k\right)

Given ans arbitrary signal x(t)x(t) we can represent sampling as multiplication by the impulse train

xs(t)=x(t)⋅xp(t)=∑n=−∞∞x(t)⋅δ(t−nT)=∑n=−∞∞x(nT)⋅δ(t−nT)x_s(t) = x(t)\cdot x_p(t) = \sum\limits_{n = -\infty}^{\infty} x(t)\cdot\delta(t-nT) = \sum\limits_{n = -\infty}^{\infty} x(nT)\cdot\delta(t-nT)

where we define x(nT)=x[n]x(nT) = x[n].

In the frequency domain, multiplication is convolution

Xs(jω)=12πXp(jω)*X(jω)=1T∑n=−∞∞X(j(ω−2πTn))X_s(j\omega) = \frac{1}{2\pi} X_p(j\omega)*X(j\omega) = \frac{1}{T} \sum\limits_{n = -\infty}^{\infty} X\left(j\left(\omega - \frac{2\pi}{T} n \right) \right)

The frequency content of the sampled signal consists of copies of the frequency content of the original signal called images.

Nyquist Criteria

To ensure there is do distortion in the signal we require the images do not overlap, which occurs if T<12BT < \frac{1}{2B}, where BB is the bandwidth of x(t)x(t). This is the Nyquist criteria. In terms of a frequency this sample rate is then ω0=2πT\omega_0 = \tfrac{2\pi}{T} radians-per-second.

If BB is finite then x(t)x(t) is and we can choose TT to meet the Nyquist rate.

Practical Sampling

While ideal sampling thoery is mathematically simple and intuitive, ideal sampling cannot be implemented in practice because we cannot generate ideal impulses for the impulse train.

We can generate short pulses that approximate the impulse however. This is done through a circuit called a sample and hold.

Reconstruction Theory

Given a DT signal x[n]x[n] and a sample spacing TT, we can define a corresponding CT signal as xp(t)=∑n=−∞∞x[n]δ(t−nT)x_p(t) = \sum\limits_{n = -\infty}^{\infty} x[n] \, \delta(t-nT) the impulse train with each impulse weighted by the DT signal.

CT signal reconstruction can be viewed from two different (but equivalent) perspectives. In the time domain perspective, the CT signal x(t)x(t) corresponding to a DT signal x[n]x[n] can be viewed as interpolation, where the values of the CT signal are equal to the DT signal at intervals of the sample time, i.e. x(nT)=x[n]x(nT) = x[n], and in between the value of x(t)x(t) is interpolated. If the interpolation is of zero-order, the value at x(nT)x(nT) is held constant until x(nT+T)x(nT+T). This is called a zero-order hold, and can mathematically modeled as convolution of the weighted impulse train with a pulse p(t)=u(t)−u(t−T)p(t) = u(t) - u(t-T) whose width is the sample time, called the interpolation function. y(t)=p(t)*xp(t)y(t) = p(t)*x_p(t)

Practical Reconstruction

As we have seen before we cannot physically represent the impulse train nor the ideal low-pass filter. Thus practical reconstruction uses an approximation of the ideal reconstruction filter by a digital-to-analog converter (DAC), followed by a causal (and thus physically possible) low-pass filter.

Reconstruction(anti-imaging) filter

The zero-order hold is followed by the reconstruction (anti-imaging) filter which low-pass filters the output and smooths-out the jumps from value to value.

With or without and anti-imaging filter the signal is positive. This can be followed by a summing amplifier to shoft the signal voltage up or down and amplify as needed.
